History

Trace the profound legacy of the Roman See, from the ancient foundations of St. Peter's martyrdom to the signing of the Lateran Treaty that shaped the modern Vatican state.

A city of faith, art, and statehood

The history of Vatican City, from St. Peter's tomb to the modern city-state

Vatican City is more than a religious center; it is one of the most influential cities in the world—a place where history, art, diplomacy, and faith have shaped the face of Europe for over two millennia.

Origins

Early traces in ancient Rome

The area that is now Vatican City grew along the Tiber River, in a place long recognized as significant in ancient Rome. According to Catholic tradition, this is where Saint Peter, the first apostle, died and was buried. From this origin, a sacred area developed into a center of pilgrimage, prayer, and spiritual authority.

“Upon the tombs of martyrs and popes, the history of the Church grew into one of the most influential forces in the world.”

A central role

A global spiritual center

As the Catholic Church expanded, the Vatican area grew into a symbol of unity in faith. St. Peter’s Basilica and the artistic treasures bequeathed by the popes made this place not only a place of worship, but also a living museum of history, architecture, and culture.

4th century

St. Peter’s Tomb

Tradition holds that this site became the burial place of Saint Peter, marking the beginning of the area’s importance in Church history.

756

Grant of land by King Pepin

Territories around Rome were given to the Pope, which became the first step toward the formation of a vast Papal State.

1929

Lateran Treaty

This agreement established Vatican City as an independent and sovereign state, separating the status of the Catholic Church from Italy.

Today

The modern city-state

Today, Vatican City is a diplomatic center, a vital place for the Catholic Church, and a global destination that unites art, history, and faith.

Why its history matters

A living legacy

The history of Vatican City is not only about a small state, but about its influence on culture, art, law, and international relations. From its magnificent basilica to the halls holding Michelangelo’s works, every corner tells a story of struggle, grandeur, and spiritual perseverance.

Artistic heritage The birthplace of major works by Michelangelo, Raphael, and other Renaissance masters.
Diplomatic role A center of international relations connecting the Church and nations across the world.
Symbol of faith Still a destination for pilgrimage and a reflection of the long history of the Church.
